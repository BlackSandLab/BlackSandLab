# Code of Conduct

## BlackSand Lab

*A hackerspace built on open source, curiosity, and the hands-on imperative.*

---

## Our Pledge

We, the members and contributors of **BlackSand Lab**, pledge to make participation in our community a respectful, inclusive, and intellectually stimulating experience for everyone — regardless of age, body size, visible or invisible disability, ethnicity, sex characteristics, gender identity and expression, level of experience, education, socio-economic status, nationality, personal appearance, race, religion, or sexual identity and orientation.

We believe that **access to knowledge, tools, and technology should be unlimited and total**. We build, we break, we learn, and we share — together.

## The 11 Laws of Hacker

These principles are adapted from the foundational hacker ethic first documented by Steven Levy in *Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution* (1984), and expanded by the Chaos Computer Club and the broader hacker community. They are not rules to be enforced, but a shared philosophy that guides how we operate.citeweb_search:1#7web_search:1#9

### 1. The Hands-On Imperative
**Access to computers — and anything which might teach you something about the way the world really works — should be unlimited and total.**

We encourage everyone to take things apart, experiment, break things (intentionally or not), and learn from the process. Curiosity is our engine. If a door is locked, we learn how locks work — not to break in, but to understand.

### 2. Information Wants to Be Free
**All information should be free.**

Knowledge increases in value when shared. We document our projects, publish our findings, open-source our code, and teach what we learn. "Free" means unrestricted access — not necessarily zero cost. We respect licenses, attribution, and the spirit of open source.citeweb_search:1#9

### 3. Mistrust Authority — Promote Decentralization
**Bureaucracy, gatekeeping, and centralization are flawed systems.**

We favor flat hierarchies, consensus-based decisions, and distributed ownership. No single person holds the keys to the kingdom. We build resilient systems — technical and social — that don't depend on one node.

### 4. Meritocracy Over Credentials
**Hackers should be judged by their hacking, not bogus criteria such as degrees, age, race, or position.**

Your code, your builds, your curiosity, and your contributions matter. Your resume does not. We value what you *do* and what you *share*, not where you studied or who you know.

### 5. Art and Beauty on a Computer
**You can create art and beauty on a computer.**

Elegant code, clever hardware hacks, and beautiful circuit designs are celebrated. We appreciate the craft. A well-structured shell script can be poetry. A clean PCB layout is a canvas.

### 6. Computers Can Change Your Life for the Better
**Technology is a force for personal and collective improvement.**

We use our skills to solve real problems, empower individuals, and make the world more interesting. Hacking is not about destruction — it's about creation, understanding, and improvement.

### 7. Do No Harm
**Above all else, do no harm.**

We do not damage systems, destroy data, or hurt people — physically, mentally, or emotionally. Pranks are allowed, but only when harmless and consensual. If you accidentally break something, you fix it.citeweb_search:1#5

### 8. Leave No Traces (Where Appropriate)
**Don't litter other people's data. Don't call unnecessary attention to yourself.**

When exploring systems (with permission), clean up after yourself. Respect others' workspaces, digital and physical. Don't leave a mess in shared environments — whether that's a server, a workbench, or a Git repository.

### 9. Make Public Data Available, Protect Private Data
**Transparency for the collective, privacy for the individual.**

We advocate for open data, open government, and open science. At the same time, we fiercely protect personal privacy. We do not dox, we do not leak private information, and we respect confidentiality agreements.citeweb_search:1#7

### 10. Share What You Know
**Teach when asked to teach. Share when you have knowledge to spread.**

Mentorship is not optional — it's the culture. Write documentation. Give workshops. Answer questions patiently. The only thing better than learning is teaching someone else.

### 11. Persevere, But Don't Be Stupid
**A hacker should persevere but not be greedy nor take stupid risks.**

We value persistence. We also value knowing when to stop, when to ask for help, and when a risk isn't worth taking. Hacking is about smart exploration, not reckless endangerment.citeweb_search:1#4

## Standards of Behavior

In addition to the 11 Laws, we expect all community members to uphold the following:

### Be Respectful
- Treat others with kindness and patience.
- No harassment, trolling, or personal attacks.
- Disagree with ideas, not people.

### Be Inclusive
- Use welcoming language.
- Be mindful of different cultural backgrounds and experience levels.
- Don't gatekeep. If someone is eager to learn, help them.

### Be Constructive
- Give feedback that helps people grow.
- Accept feedback gracefully.
- Focus on solutions, not blame.

### Be Responsible
- Follow local laws and our lab's safety guidelines.
- Don't hack systems you don't have permission to access.
- Report vulnerabilities responsibly — don't exploit them.

### Be Open
- Default to open source when possible.
- Document your work so others can build on it.
- Credit your sources and collaborators.

## Unacceptable Behavior

The following behaviors are considered unacceptable in the BlackSand Lab community:

- **Harassment** of any kind, including sexual harassment, intimidation, or stalking.
- **Discrimination** based on the characteristics listed in Our Pledge.
- **Doxxing** or sharing private information without consent.
- **Malicious hacking** — unauthorized access to systems, data theft, or destruction.
- **Plagiarism** or taking credit for others' work.
- **Disruptive behavior** that prevents others from learning, building, or participating.
- **Unsafe practices** that endanger people or equipment.

---

## Enforcement

Instances of abusive, harassing, or otherwise unacceptable behavior may be reported to the community moderators or lab stewards. All complaints will be reviewed and investigated promptly and fairly.

Consequences for violations may include:
- A private warning
- Temporary suspension from community spaces
- Permanent expulsion from the lab and revocation of access

Moderators have the right and responsibility to remove, edit, or reject contributions that are not aligned with this Code of Conduct, and will communicate reasons for moderation decisions when appropriate.

---

## Scope

This Code of Conduct applies within all community spaces — Discord, GitHub, physical lab space, events, and any other forum where you represent BlackSand Lab. It also applies when an individual is officially representing the community in public spaces.
